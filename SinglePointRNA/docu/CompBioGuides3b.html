After ten cycles of the golden search we get to an interval smaller than 0.001 and the maximum value of cell growth is reached at a sugar concentration of 93.6%. This will be enough detail to set the best nutrient concentration for bacterial growth in our medium.

Simple differentiable functions like the one in the example above can also be optimized manually using calculus, but often times well find ourselves with complex models, or with more than one input variable, which can be optimized with other methods such as Gradient Descent

4: Stochastic Algorithms

As we have seen in the last section, when a scoring function can be derivated, we can use that gradient to find and follow the trend of a function towards a maximum or minimum. But what do we do if our scoring function:

  • Would take way too much time or computational power to be optimized by deterministic methods.
  • Has multiple local optima that would be good enough for our needs and easier to find.
  • The target function we want to optimize is susceptible to noise or has too many local maximums or minimums, so deterministic methods might have trouble getting stuck.

In cases like this we can benefit from optimization methods that include some randomness into the process. This randomness can be achieved through several ways, like drawing parameter values from a probabilistic distribution, or making small random changes in each iteration.

This means that given the same data, stochastic algorithms might explore different possibilities in every run. These algorithms wont test the whole range of possible solutions exhaustively, and dont offer guarantees that the solution found is a global optimum, but their heuristic approach makes partial solutions available for complex situations that cant be optimized in a reasonable time with other methods.

Well illustrate briefly with an example the basis of some of the most popular stochastic optimization methods: evolutionary algorithms.

Evolutionary algorithms aim to replicate the success of natural selection in finding good enough solutions. As many optimization algorithms, evolutionary algorithms are iterative in nature: part of their structure is a cycle repeated time and time again until the models are good enough for the user, or a limit is reached.

This iterative structure is very reminiscent of how living organisms reproduce, and change every generation as the better adapted populations thrive and others dwindle. How can we reproduce this selection process in an optimization algorithm? Evolutionary algorithms often follow this steps:

  • They are population based algorithms: since the start, we will have a set of possible solutions to the function we want to optimize.
  • In each generation the different solutions compete: the possible solutions will be evaluated, the best ones will be kept, and the worst, discarded
  • Each cycle, the new generation of possible solutions if built from the best performing possibilities of the last cycle.
  • In order to introduce variation and avoid getting stuck in a local optimum, evolutionary algorithms adapt mechanisms such as recombinations or mutations.

Lets say we are developing an early diagnostic protocol for a disease:

- In the literature available there are described numerous polymorphisms linked to this disease to varying degrees, but all of them are also present in the general population.

- Besides a list of polymorphisms, we have an independent sequencing study done in a patient cohort as well as healthy subjects, that indicates if a particular polymorphism is present or not.

- Our specific goal would be to develop a predictive model from polymorphism data, so that is possible to detect susceptible individuals by sequencing a few small specific regions of the genome. If the treatment is not innocuous on healthy subjects, we would need to heavily penalize false positives. Additionally, depending on how costly the sequencing process is, models that need fewer polymorphisms to make accurate predictions would be favored. All of this limitations are what define our scoring function

In the chart above theres a summary of the optimization procedure, with the sources of stochastic variation in green:

  • First, to create our initial generation of predictive models, we can take several random samples of the polymorphisms linked to our disease.
  • Then, using the sequencing study and our scoring function, we evaluate the first generation of models and select the best of them.
  • The second generation will be generated by randomly combining (recombining) polymorphisms used by the best parents.
  • Some of the children models will be altered further to introduce some additional variation (mutation).
  • The second generation is evaluated, and the cycle goes on.

Over time the models will end up converging into a combination of polymorphisms that are well adapted to the conditions of our scoring function: detect at risk people, avoid false positives, and use a small number of polymorphisms.
